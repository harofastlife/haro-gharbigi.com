Music & Creative Interests

Sound, rhythm, and artistic expression that fuel innovation.

Music isn't background noise. It's a creative catalyst that shapes mood, sparks ideas, and provides the rhythm for productive work. From film scores that inspire strategic thinking to energetic tracks that fuel long coding sessions, sound is an integral part of the creative process.

The Soundtrack of Work

Building technology companies requires sustained focus across long hours. Music creates the environment that makes deep work possible. Different tasks demand different sonic landscapes: ambient electronics for concentrated coding, classical compositions for writing and strategy, and driving rock for pushing through challenging deadlines.

Film scores hold a special place in the rotation. Composers like Hans Zimmer, Ennio Morricone, and Johann Johannsson create soundscapes that evoke emotion without distraction. The Interstellar soundtrack can turn a routine task into something that feels meaningful. The Blade Runner 2049 score adds weight to any moment of contemplation.

Eclectic Tastes

Musical preferences refuse easy categorization. Classic rock provides foundation: Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd, The Rolling Stones. These artists understood that technical mastery serves emotional expression, not the other way around.

Electronic and ambient music offer tools for focus and exploration. Artists like Brian Eno pioneered the concept of ambient music as "furniture" for the mind. Modern electronic producers continue pushing boundaries, creating sounds that didn't exist a decade ago.

World music expands perspective. Traditional instruments and scales from different cultures remind us that our default assumptions aren't universal. Middle Eastern music, in particular, connects to cultural roots while offering something genuinely different from Western pop structures.

Music and Memory

Certain songs become time machines. The opening notes transport you back to specific moments: working on a breakthrough feature, driving through Los Angeles at night, celebrating a major milestone. Building a personal soundtrack means creating anchors for significant memories.

This connection between sound and memory has practical applications. Associating specific playlists with specific types of work creates mental shortcuts. When the familiar opening track plays, the brain knows it's time to focus.

Creative Cross-Pollination

The best ideas often come from unexpected connections. Music from different eras and cultures provides raw material for creative synthesis. A chord progression might inspire a user interface approach. A rhythmic pattern might suggest a new way to structure a presentation.

Los Angeles amplifies these connections. The city's music scene spans every genre, from intimate jazz clubs to massive arena shows. Live music offers something recordings can't: the energy of shared experience, the spontaneity of improvisation, the reminder that behind every sound is human creativity.

Beyond Listening

Understanding music means appreciating the craft behind it. How did that producer achieve that sound? What makes that melody memorable? Why does that rhythm feel so compelling? These questions mirror the curiosity that drives technology innovation.

Music production itself offers lessons for technology entrepreneurs. Both fields require balancing technical precision with creative vision. Both demand continuous learning as tools evolve. Both reward those who can synthesize influences into something original.

The Role of Silence

Appreciation for music includes appreciation for its absence. Strategic silence creates space for reflection and prevents creative burnout. The contrast between sound and silence makes both more meaningful.

Some of the best thinking happens in quiet moments: early morning before the world wakes up, late evening when the day's noise subsides. These silent intervals allow ideas to surface that might otherwise be drowned out.

This balance extends to life beyond work. Times for energetic engagement alternate with times for contemplative rest. The rhythm of creativity requires both sound and silence.
